Imageomics platform redesign

Website & Platform Development · Imageomics Institute · NSF HDR (five institutes, public-facing)

Public systems don’t usually fail all at once. They slowly become harder to use until people stop trusting them.

I worked on this platform through Imageomics. While Imageomics owned and maintained the site, it served the full NSF HDR community across five research institutes and was open to the public.

Researchers, faculty, staff, and external visitors used the site to find shared resources, understand programs, and submit requests. Over time, the structure stopped holding up.

I rebuilt the platform using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. The goal wasn’t a visual refresh. It was clarity, accuracy, and something the institute could maintain safely over time.

Context

Imageomics hosted and maintained a public-facing platform on behalf of the NSF HDR program.

The site needed to work for multiple audiences at once: internal teams across five institutes and members of the public looking for clear, reliable information.

The redesign required migrating all content from the legacy site while ensuring accuracy, accessibility, and formal approval.

Problem

The issue wasn’t missing features. It was accumulated mess.

  • Navigation reflected program history instead of user intent
  • Similar content was split across multiple sections
  • Approval steps weren’t clearly documented
  • Public and internal needs blurred together

Users

  • Researchers across five institutes using shared resources
  • Faculty tracking approvals and timelines
  • Administrative staff coordinating requests and documentation
  • Public visitors learning about the program and its work

My role

I worked with a student designer. I handled the frontend, content migration, and how the system actually behaved.

  • Turned designs into a working, maintainable site
  • Migrated all content from the legacy platform
  • Worked through approvals before publishing anything publicly
  • Reorganized content around real workflows and audiences
  • Shipped changes and adjusted based on feedback

Migration often meant reconciling outdated or conflicting content with stakeholders instead of copying pages over blindly.

Constraints

  • One platform serving public and internal audiences
  • Accuracy and approval mattered more than speed
  • Limited engineering support
  • The site needed to survive long-term handoff

Approach

  • Listed common tasks by audience
  • Separated public information from internal workflows
  • Grouped content around intent, not initiatives
  • Shipped in small steps to avoid disruption

Structure before and after

The original navigation grew around individual programs and initiatives. As more work was added, top-level options multiplied and similar content ended up split across sections.

I restructured the navigation around what people actually came to do. Related work was grouped under shared categories, and program-specific content moved one level down. This reduced top-level choices and made the site easier to scan without losing depth.

Implementation

  • Vanilla HTML, CSS, and JavaScript
  • No frameworks
  • JavaScript only where it helped
  • Simple structure, easy to maintain

Impact

  • Less time spent searching for information
  • Approvals moved faster
  • Fewer clarification emails
  • The site became the default public reference

Tradeoffs

  • Chose clarity over deep customization
  • Kept manual review to protect public accuracy
  • Delayed advanced features

What could’ve gone wrong

  • Publishing inaccurate public information
  • Breaking links relied on by external users
  • Blurring lines between public and internal content

Approval flow

  • Identify content to move or update
  • Check accuracy with staff
  • Get faculty or program lead sign-off
  • Publish publicly

What I’d do next

I’d focus on search and onboarding, then let usage data guide any bigger changes.

Reflection

  • Public-facing systems raise the stakes
  • Migration is where trust is earned
  • Structure matters more than features

Built slowly. Improved continuously.
